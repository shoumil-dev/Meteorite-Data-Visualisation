Meteorite Landings on Earth

Meteorites are space rocks that fall to Earth’s surface. Meteorites are the last stage in the existence of these type of space rocks. Before they were meteorites, the rocks were meteors. Before they were meteors, they were meteoroids. Meteoroids are lumps of rock or metal that orbit the sun. Meteoroids become meteors when they crash into Earth’s atmosphere and the gases surrounding them briefly light up as “shooting stars.” While most meteors burn up and disintegrate in the atmosphere, many of these space rocks reach Earth’s surface in the form of meteorites. Dust-sized particles called micrometeorites make up 99 percent of the approximately 50 tons of space debris that falls on the Earth’s surface every day.

Increased awareness of meteorites in the early 1960s was caused by the Apollo program which brought meteorites to the forefront of public awareness. This was because astronauts brought back lunar meteorites to Earth for the first time. This led to an increase in people reporting meteorites they found, which helped to increase the number of meteorites discovered. Additionally, new search techniques like metal detectors and aerial surverys allowed finding of meteorites that had been missed in the past. This, coupled with increased funding for meteorite research and recovery programs led to the massive boom in meteorites discovered in the 1970s.

Fallen vs Found Meteorites

It is estimated that only 2.42% of meteorites that fall to Earth are ever found. This is because most meteorites fall into the ocean, which covers about 71% of the planet. Additionally, many meteorites fall into remote areas, such as deserts and forests, where they are difficult to find. The fact that only 2.42% of meteorites have fallen to Earth, and 97.58 of them have been found is a testament to the dedication and hard work of meteorite hunters and scientists. Meteorites in Oceans vs Land

From the doughnut chart, we can see that 83.73% of meteorites are found on land, compared to a significantly smaller 16.27% in the world's oceans. This is likely due to the fact that less than 5% of the ocean floor has been mapped. So it is likely that there are many still lying on the seabed waiting to be discovered. Additionally, meteorites in the ocean are more difficult to spot, blending in with the water and the seabed, causing many smaller meteorites to go unnoticed.

Meteorites Discovered Over Time

From the data, we can see that the number of meteorites discovered in recent times is significantly higher than in previous years. This trend can be attributed to the massive advancements in technology, making it substantially easier to locate and document meteorites that have reached the Earth's surface. The proliferation of high resolution smartphones, cameras with GPS capabilities, and easy access to the internet has empowered amateur astronomers and meteorite enthusiasts to quickly capture and share meteorite falls or finds.

Another critical factor is the size of the meteorites in question. Smaller meteorites, which are more numerous than their larger counterparts, could have easily gone unnoticed or lost to the passage of time, never to be recorded. It is only in recent years, with the increasing awareness of these smaller meteorites and more effective search methods, that we've started to capture a more comprehensive picture of the meteorite population on Earth.

What are the Types of Meteorite Classes?

Analyzing recclass data for meteorites over the years provides valuable insights into the composition and diversity of extraterrestrial materials that have reached Earth. The recclass classification system categorizes meteorites based on their mineralogical and chemical characteristics, offering a comprehensive understanding of their origins and formation processes. By tracking changes in recclass data over time, scientists can uncover trends in meteorite composition, identify rare or unusual meteorite types, and gain insights into the dynamic processes occurring in the solar system.
